Establish a pace and watch the speedometer. If the speedometer breaks, slow down and fix the issue. If it looks good, accelerate.

This framework helps facilitate the classic boardroom tension — investors pushing for rapid scale with little regard for organizational capabilities, and executives pumping the brakes to ensure goal achievement (and preserve their jobs).

At Stage 2 Capital, we call it Stay-or-Go-or-Slow.

We still encourage an annual plan. But we challenge the exec team and board to pre-agree on the following before the quarter begins:

--> STAY: What would we see that validates staying the course?

--> GO: What would we see by end of next quarter that would justify going faster than plan (and increasing the hiring pace)?

--> SLOW: What would we see that would justify slowing down — reducing or pausing hiring until the scale issue is diagnosed and resolved?

At each quarterly check-in, we:

Evaluate where we landed (Stay-or-Go-or-Slow)
--> Adjust the hiring plan accordingly
--> Update the forward-looking 4-quarter plan

The result: we align growth with the current capabilities of the business — instead of waiting for the next fiscal year to reset.

A couple nuances that matter.

Boards can be hesitant because they worry about growth rates falling below IRR expectations. But successful startup exits rarely happen in the first 2–3 years. They take 5–10 years. Acquirers won't ask about the Q2-to-Q3 growth rate from 8 years ago. They care about the most recent ~8 quarters at most.

And adjusting the forward plan doesn't mean lowering accountability. Keep tracking against the original plan for performance management (and comp) purposes — otherwise teams hide behind the revised forecast. This isn't about giving the team an accountability "out." It's about managing the business using the latest information we have.
