
I had the opportunity to speak at Underscore VC last week — thank you to Lily Lyman and Brian Devaney for hosting and for the warm support of The Science of Scaling. Really sharp group of founders in the room.

The question that sparked the best conversation: how do you transition from founder selling to a professional go-to-market org?

There's a lot of content out there on this - codify the process, hire someone to build the playbook, etc. But one thing that doesn't get talked about enough is the pacing of how a founder actually pulls back from the funnel.

Here's what I see happen too often: a founder spends 80% of their time selling in year one. Gets to product market fit. Closes 15 customers. Raises the round. Then immediately drops to 0% selling. Two bad quarters follow, and the momentum the business had worked so hard to build is gone.

I don't think that tradeoff is worth it.

Instead of going from 80% to 0%, consider pulling back to something like 40%. Give yourself room to build the team and the infrastructure — but don't abandon the pipeline entirely in the process.

The other mistake I see: founders using their first reps as glorified SDRs while they play super rep. Let your new reps build their own pipeline. They need to scrape their knees. They need to go through the learnings. They need to be held accountable to their own numbers. The moment you're running pipeline for them, you've undermined the whole thing.
