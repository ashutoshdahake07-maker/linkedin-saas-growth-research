I met with 178 AI founders and GTM leaders over two days in SF last week through coffees, dinners, and small-group workshops. Here are the themes that stood out:

1. AI adoption is moving from experimentation to accountability.
Companies are pulling back from bottoms-up AI experimentation and moving toward centralized ownership in Operations. The focus is shifting to controlled pilots, measurable business value, ROI accountability, and eliminating redundant investments.

2. "Proof of Outcome" is replacing "Proof of Concept."
Buyers care less about technical feasibility and more about business impact. Leading sales teams are increasingly aligning around defined business outcomes, 90-day success metrics, and in some cases, money-back guarantees to overcome ROI skepticism. This is not outcome-base pricing though. 

3. In-person GTM is one spotlight of increasing effectiveness.
For non-PLG motions, companies are seeing strong results from executive dinners, city-by-city events, and disciplined trade show strategies. Interestingly, many teams are lowering the ACV threshold for in-person meetings. AI helps offset the traditional productivity tradeoffs of field selling by keeping reps productive while traveling.

4. Hyper-personalization is becoming table stakes.
AI is enabling teams to truly deliver on the "market-of-1" promise with prospecting materials, demo environments, business cases, and collateral at scale. The result is higher engagement and shorter sales cycles.

5. Enablement is becoming a major bottleneck.
Product velocity is accelerating faster than many GTM teams can absorb. The challenge is no longer building capabilities—it's ensuring sellers understand and can effectively communicate them.

6. AEO → MCP is the new SEO → PLG, with the same risks and upside.
Like PLG last decade, some companies will overapply it while others will ignore it. The key question is whether it fits your product-market context. If it does, moving early creates a meaningful distribution advantage. If it doesn't, forcing it can be equally costly. Bottom line, if there is a chance it fits your context, you have to try it.

7. The best AI-era interview question focuses on process, not answers.
Using the calculator analogy, the strongest candidates aren't distinguished by getting the answer—they're distinguished by understanding the process. Ask candidates to solve a problem with AI, then explain their reasoning, assumptions, and approach. This quickly separates first-principles thinkers from copy-and-paste executors. As AI becomes ubiquitous, understanding how someone thinks may become more valuable than understanding whether they can generate an answer.
