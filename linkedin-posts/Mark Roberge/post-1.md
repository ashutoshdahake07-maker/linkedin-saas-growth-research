First Mover or Fast Follower. Which would you prefer to be?

Last week, I spoke at one of the OpenClaw AI hacking events at MIT. These gatherings are typically packed with undergraduate entrepreneurs building AI products at an incredible pace.

One trend caught my attention: a growing belief that the AI era may favor the fast follower more than ever before.

For years, the startup ecosystem has arguably overweighted first-mover advantage. And to be fair, it is real. First movers often earn the first headlines, attract top engineering talent, secure early customers, and raise the most capital.

But there's another side to the story.

First movers also spend enormous amounts of capital figuring out the product, educating the market, and evangelizing an entirely new category. As a result, fast followers have historically won 2/3 of the time and first movers 1/3 (e.g., Slack, Zoom, Google, and Salesforce were not the first companies in their respective categories).

The students I spoke with believe the pendulum has swung even further toward fast followers in today's AI market. Their argument centered on four points:

1) Many AI category leaders have raised capital at massive valuation multiples. As a result, they are under tremendous pressure to grow into those valuations and often find themselves in what one founder called "ACV jail." Lowering prices becomes difficult when revenue expectations are so high.

2) AI coding tools have dramatically accelerated the ability to replicate products. Fast followers can now identify what actually works, build similar functionality much faster, and potentially offer it at a 70% lower price point.

3) Many first movers built their technology architecture and organizational structures before the latest wave of AI innovations. New entrants can start with a more modern stack and operating model from day one.

4) First movers are over-indexing on ARR growth at the expense of a durable moat and high LTV customer base, making them vulnerable

The obvious question is: What's stopping someone from doing the same thing to the fast followers?

Their response: Unlike the first movers, they aren't raising at extreme valuations. They aren't in "ACV jail". They're building on modern infrastructure from the start, and implementing the necessary agility to take advantage of future operational efficiencies, passing them to the end customer. Yes, another company could come after them with a lower-cost alternative, but perhaps only 5-10% cheaper—which may not be compelling enough for customers to switch.

Who knows?

History suggests first-mover advantage is often overrated. The next few years may reveal whether AI pushes that trend even further—or whether the AI native big names defend their lead.
