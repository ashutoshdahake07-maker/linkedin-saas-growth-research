Most people are thinking about AI backwards.

They ask their GPT-variant to produce the answer to the problem, thinking it can replace an expert.

Then they spend more time 2nd-guessing the output than they would have spent doing the work themselves...
If you can't assess whether AI is producing a good answer, you're overextending its capabilities.

Flip it.

Last week I needed to audit a Google Ads account I hadn't seen before. I didn't ask AI to tell me what was wrong. 
I prompted it to walk me through the diagnostic sequence a senior practitioner would run:

- Which reports to pull, in which order
- What each one tells you that the next one doesn't
- Which signals overlap before drawing a conclusion

Then I pulled the data and did the thinking. I critically evaluate whether the process was wrong or the conclusions. Of course I spotted some errors - the same ones I'd expect a smart junior to make (unlike a smart junior AI occasionally provides fresh insights I've genuinely never encountered before).

The synthesis of everything written about Google Ads into one queryable layer is the real value in my view. Not expecting AI to produce the answer itself as much as the aggregate context that helps improve the quality of the answer I produce.

The skill that matters now is knowing enough about the domain to recognise when the model has handed you a clean diagnostic path vs. a confident hallucination. 
Then, double-check the individual components.

The common argument is that AI commoditises expertise "now anyone can do what an expert does, so experts are worth less."

I reckon it didn't get cheaper. The market value of genuine expertise hasn't dropped. 
If anything, the floor for "good work" has risen, because confident-sounding mediocre output is now infinite and free. Being able to tell good from bad is rarer and more valuable, not less.

An expert who used to audit one Google Ads account a week can now audit five, because AI compresses the mechanical parts (knowing which reports, in what order, what to look for). The expertise itself is the fulcrum; AI is the longer lever arm.

The expert's judgement now moves more weight per unit of time.

Unfortunately, we live in a culture where a confident-sounding answer is more satisfactory than saying 'I don't know'. The error rate has gone through the roof - and not just with the volume of LinkedIn AI-slop posts & comments - but poor understanding and expectations of proper AI use.
