Instead of worrying about AI taking people's jobs (some will lose theirs, some won't, most jobs will evolve) the smarter question is: how do you hire one?

AI pricing is in flux:
- We've got the subscription & API models from the large language vendors
- We've got outcome-based pricing (intercom, sierra)
- We've got 'hire an agent' (relevant ai, aisdr)

As companies build out on top of the infrastructure provided by large language model (LLM) providers, they'll customise skills & develop workflows.

Third-party companies will experiment until they figure out combinations that work for different marketing / sales workflows (from account building to ICP modelling to lead nurturing sequences).

The market will progressively shift to paying a fixed fee for an AI agent similar to how employees or contractors are paid. Hiring people + AI agents, and sometimes buying hybrid teams (I'm excluding operators here).

Because these AI agents will conduct a variety of specialised tasks on an ongoing basis - paying by token or outcome won't be viable as the costs will be too high.

Outcome works when it's something like resolving a customer support query. But when it comes to marketing activity, it's harder to measure short-term effects.

Procurement teams aren't quite equipped for this.
Neither is HR.

No job spec (more like customisation).
No probation period (more like SaaS trial).
No (traditional) performance reviews. 

We're still in the infancy of the commercialisation of AI. Most companies won't build their own complex AI agents, just as they don't build their own software but buy existing SaaS solutions.

Software will get cheaper - and eventually end up being entirely commoditised (Google say 70% of its code is now AI-generated). But planning, architecturing, maintaining and improving software is still a pain - and if it isn't core to a company's differentiator, it'll be outsourced.

Google ads / SEO / LinkedIn ads etc. are all still outsourced despite being commoditised and are all considerably easier than deploying software.
Every company will have licensed some generalised AI capability, but specialised-capabilities won't be served by the same companies that run the LLMs.
