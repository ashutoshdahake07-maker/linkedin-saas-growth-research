Here's a great example from this week that personifies what 'brand' really means.

The term 'brand' is thrown around a lot. It has lots of intellectual definitions, but the true one is deeper; it's experiential.

An app for LinkedIn analytics got shut down (scraping data or automations etc' break T&Cs). The app served a need LinkedIn didn't provide - clear, easy-to-use analytics.

A competitor brand slagged them off and got a ton of backlash. The backlash was due to people having loved it.
They loved...an inanimate software. A bit odd when you think about it, no?
You could say they loved the outcome shield provided and the feelings associated with that experience.

(All this is playing out regardless of legal ramifications, funny enough)

My favourite software of all time was 3D Studio Max. I spent hours losing track of time, honing skills, creating worlds of fantasy from scratch. I associated it with Autodesk, and as such, loved the brand.
Because positive experience + utility + repeat usage = emotional attachment a lot of the time.

If your company isn't moving people emotionally, you don't have a brand. You just have a logo. Crafting a successful brand is creating the conditions for people to fall in love with the experience your company provides.
