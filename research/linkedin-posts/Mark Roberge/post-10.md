
One of the biggest shifts I expect in the AI era is a reversal of extreme sales specialization—and the return of the full-cycle salesperson.

Specialization made sense when two conditions were true:

1- A portion of the end-to-end workflow required a hard-to-find, high-skill capability.
In sales, that skill was converting an opportunity into a customer. If you found someone great at that, you didn’t want them spending time on lower-skill tasks.

2- Different stages of the workflow required meaningfully different skill sets, making role-level optimization attractive.

But specialization has real costs.

When SDRs are tasked with only setting meetings, you risk a lot of bad meetings.
When sellers are tasked with only acquiring customers, you risk a lot of bad customers.
An entire body of GTM process, compensation, and handoff design emerged just to realign these fragmented roles around a single outcome.

AI changes the optimization formula.

By automating large portions of the workflow—and lowering the skill required to execute many tasks—the costs of specialization now outweigh the benefits. As a result, I believe we’ll move back toward full-cycle reps who:

• Generate their own meetings
• Convert meetings into customers
• Renew and expand accounts

And this shift isn’t limited to sales.

We’re seeing a similar pattern in R&D, where traditional PM / Designer / Front-End Eng / Back-End Eng / Data Scientist roles are converging back toward the full-cycle engineer.
Longer term, AI may let us rethink organizational design altogether.

Many functional silos exist because of human cognitive limits. It’s rare for someone trained in Finance to write code—and vice versa. But as AI augments our capabilities, those boundaries blur. Finance can move closer to Sales. Product closer to Support. Organizational structured become more cross-functional. (Yes—maybe advise your kids to double major 😉)

B2C Growth teams offer an interesting blueprint. When implemented well, they were small, cross-functional teams optimized around the entire customer journey.
Perhaps the biggest innovator’s dilemma for incumbents—and opportunity for native AI startups—isn’t product or pricing, but how they structure and staff their organizations.
