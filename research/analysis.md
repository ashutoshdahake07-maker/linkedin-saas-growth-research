Analysis: 150 LinkedIn Posts from 10 B2B SaaS Growth Experts
Analyst: Ashutosh Dahake
 Date: June 2026 
Corpus: 150 posts (15 each) from Neil Patel, April Dunford, Craig Hewitt, Jason M. Lemkin, Lara Acosta, Mark Roberge, Oren Greenberg, Sangram Vajre, Steli Efti, and Tomasz Tunguz.
 Window: ~May–June 2026.

A note on the data before we start
I started by reading every post. Two things are important before you read this analysis:
The repository contains text transcriptions rather than the original posts, so I couldn't determine whether a post was a carousel, video, or text from metadata alone. I deduced format from contextual cues within the text (e.g., "in this video," "the chart below," "graphic below"). Wherever I'm making that interpretation, I've stated it explicitly. I also chose not to repeat the README's "6.6% carousel" statistic, because that figure doesn't appear anywhere in the 150 posts. It's a claim, not an observation from the dataset.
The dataset also doesn't include engagement metrics like saves, DMs, or profile views, apart from a few isolated examples (e.g., Lara mentioning impressions). So the discussion of engagement signals is based on what the creators explicitly say they optimize for, together with the only genuinely data-backed example in the dataset (Mark Roberge's Post 3). I rely on those rather than introducing unsupported KPIs.
That's the honest frame. Now the patterns.

Pattern 1: AI isn't the topic-it's the backdrop. Around 85% of posts reference it.
This is the most dominant pattern in the dataset, and it outweighs every other theme. Regardless of their area of expertise, nearly every creator frames their content through the lens of AI:
April Dunford (positioning): "Positioning in the Age of AI" and how AI reshapes market positioning.
Jason Lemkin (SaaS metrics): "1.25 humans + 20 AI agents closed 140% of what our all-human sales team did."
Steli Efti (sales): 7 of 15 posts focus on the launch of Chloe, an AI voice agent.
Oren Greenberg (demand generation): AEO, LLM economics, and the claim that bots account for 57.5% of web traffic.
Tomasz Tunguz (venture capital): Inference pricing, open models, and what he calls the "harness era."
Sangram Vajre (ABM/GTM): Repeatedly argues that AI amplifies strong GTM strategies but doesn't fix weak ones.
Mark Roberge (sales): AI-driven changes to sales teams-the return of full-cycle reps, the decline of SDR roles, and "Proof of Outcome" replacing "Proof of Concept."
My takeaway: In this period, having an AI angle is no longer a differentiator-it's the baseline. The creators who stand out don't simply argue that AI is important; they make a specific, sometimes contrarian, claim about how AI changes the rules within their domain. Broad AI optimism blends into the background, while concrete, opinionated arguments are far more distinctive.

Pattern 2: The creators split into two groups-insight-first and offer-first. They're playing two different games.
One thing that stood out to me is that the README's "60% problem, 25% solution, 15% product" split doesn't really hold up across the dataset. The mix changes depending on the type of creator.
The first group is insight-first creators-people like April, Tomasz, Oren, Jason, Mark, and Neil. They lead with an idea or a strong point of view, and any promotion comes almost as an afterthought: a newsletter link in the comments, a podcast link, or a book mention at the very end. April Dunford goes through around 15 posts with almost no direct product promotion, and Tomasz spends most of his posts explaining topics like inference economics before quietly adding a link at the bottom.
The second group is offer-first creators-Lara, Sangram, and Steli. For them, the content itself is part of the sales funnel:
Lara Acosta ends roughly 60% of her posts with a direct CTA-"DM me PLAYBOOK," leaving a comment, joining a cohort, or a sponsored collaboration.
Sangram Vajre promotes the GTM OS, GTMarketplace, roundtables, or fractional programs in about 70% of his posts.
Steli Efti spends around half his posts launching or promoting Chloe through testimonials, beta results, or direct product links.
My takeaway: There isn't one universal content formula. There's a spectrum-from creators who build trust first and sell quietly, to creators who intentionally use content as a sales engine. Both approaches work, but for different audiences. If you copied Lara's level of promotion into April's content, it would probably hurt credibility. If Lara adopted April's low-key approach, she'd likely generate far fewer leads. So before copying someone's strategy, you first need to understand which game they're actually playing.

Pattern 3: The dominant format is long-form text-which doesn't match what the README suggests.
Based on cues within the text, this is how the 150 posts seem to break down:
Around 70% are text-led. Most are 300–700 word posts built around a clear argument with numbered points. That's the style I consistently saw from April, Jason, Mark, Tomasz, Oren, Sangram, and Craig. They're writing mini-essays, not short captions.
About 20% combine text with a visual. These reference a chart, graphic, or carousel within the text. Tomasz does this frequently ("the chart shows..."). Mark's third post discusses data from a study, and Sangram often refers to graphics or screenshots.
Roughly 10% appear to be video posts. I inferred this from phrases like "in this video" or "watch the video." Neil Patel uses this style regularly, Lara often asks people to watch or repost a video, and Craig occasionally references YouTube.
My takeaway: The README's claim that carousels outperform everything else isn't something I could verify from this dataset. What I consistently observed was that well-structured, long-form text is the dominant format, especially among operators and investors. The only post that actually supports a format recommendation with data is Mark's third post (discussed in detail below), and its findings-more list items, more paragraph breaks, fewer hashtags, fewer links-describe exactly the writing style the strongest creators here use. So if I had to trust one signal over another, I'd trust the one backed by data inside the corpus over a claim that isn't reflected in the posts themselves.

Pattern 4: Almost every strong post uses the same "reframe" structure.
This was the most repeatable structural pattern in the entire dataset. The strongest posts follow the same flow:
Name a common belief the reader holds.
Challenge it-explain why it's wrong or no longer true.
Explain the real mechanic driving the outcome, ideally with a specific number, name, or source.
End with a clear takeaway the reader can act on.
Examples across the corpus:
Steli: "More info = more sales is a convenient lie." → buyers want clarity, not data.
Jason: "When growth slows, is it Sales' fault or the Product's fault? The answer has changed." → it's product velocity now.
Sangram: "Most B2B founders don't have a marketing strategy problem. They have an operating system problem."
Craig: "Your CRM is a liability, not an asset."
Oren: "Most people are thinking about AI backwards." → don't ask AI for the answer, ask it for the diagnostic sequence.
Mark: "First-mover advantage is often overrated." → fast followers win about two-thirds of the time.
Neil: "An open means someone saw a subject line. That's it." → track click-through, not opens.
My takeaway: If I had to study one thing from this corpus, it'd be how consistently the hook picks a fight with a default assumption. The reframe is the engine behind almost every high-performing post here.

Pattern 5: Specificity is what builds trust. Vague posts barely exist here.
One thing that stood out across the dataset is how often creators back up their point with something concrete-a number, a company, a person, or a real example. They rarely make broad statements without evidence.
Jason: "$285 billion in software market cap evaporated" over a 48-hour period.
Steli: 780,915 calls made, 104,251 real conversations, 6,424 hours spent calling.
Tomasz: Databricks at $6.9 billion ARR growing 80%, versus Snowflake at $5.3 billion growing 34%.
Lara: $27,890 generated from a single offer in 28 days.
Sangram: 70% of CEOs at PE-backed companies are replaced during the hold period; 55% of those replacements are unplanned.
Mark: Met with 178 AI founders and GTM leaders over two days in San Francisco.
What I found interesting is that Sangram states this principle explicitly in one of his publishing checklists: he asks whether a post includes at least one specific number, name, or concrete detail, because that's what builds trust. Across the rest of the dataset, that advice holds up remarkably well.
My takeaway: The strongest posts don't rely on generic statements. They support their arguments with concrete evidence. The more specific the detail, the more credible the post feels.

Pattern 6: The strongest creators build named frameworks, not random tips.
Another pattern I kept seeing: the best creators package their ideas into named systems instead of posting disconnected advice. Those frameworks become something they can refer back to again and again.
Lara: the SLAY Framework and the 4-3-2-1 Framework (four posts a week, three content pillars, two audiences, one lead magnet).
Sangram: the GTM OS and its eight connected pillars, the "fractional valley of death," and the freelancer-vs-fractional distinction.
Mark: the Science of Scaling, "Stay-or-Go-or-Slow," and the speedometer pacing model.
April: her positioning process (alternatives → unique capabilities → value themes) and "Positioning Jujitsu."
Tomasz: cost-plus vs. value-based pricing, "skill distillation," and the "harness."
Steli: recurring phrases like "idiotic optimization" and "pain and discomfort are the signal."
These frameworks do three things well. They're easy to remember. They're reusable, so the creator can bring them into dozens of posts without sounding repetitive. And over time, they become associated with that creator. Lara says this directly: "Repetition builds reputation."
My takeaway: The strongest creators aren't producing 15 unrelated pieces of advice. They're building two or three core frameworks and consistently creating content around them.

Pattern 7: The one data-backed finding (Mark Roberge's Post 3)
Most of my analysis is based on patterns I observed across the 150 posts. This finding is different-it's the only one supported by external, large-scale data, so I trust it more than anything else in the corpus.
The source is Mark Roberge's third post, which cites Arjun Moorthy's study of 6,753 LinkedIn posts from 39 B2B influencers. Key takeaways:
Post consistently, not daily. Posting more than 1–2 times a week can reduce average engagement. Quality beats quantity.
Use structured posts. Top-performing content includes 4–7× more list items and more paragraph breaks, making it easier to scan.
Limit hashtags and outbound links. High-performing insight posts use fewer hashtags and avoid unnecessary links. Links work better for promotional content.
Only use @mentions to give credit. Tagging people unnecessarily is associated with weaker performance.
My takeaway: This is the section I trust most, because it's backed by 6,753 posts rather than just my reading. If I had to build a content strategy using only evidence-backed findings, I'd focus on consistent posting, structured writing, fewer hashtags, careful use of links, and @mentions only when genuinely needed.

What was surprisingly rare
A few things hardly appeared across the dataset:
Deep tactical SEO or PPC content. Neil Patel and Oren Greenberg touch these areas, but mostly at a strategic altitude rather than step-by-step execution.
Practical fundraising advice. Jason Lemkin and Tomasz Tunguz discuss investor behavior and market trends, but not how to actually raise.
Anything not framed through AI. Almost every topic is viewed through an AI lens.
One opportunity I noticed: there's very little high-quality, non-AI operating content. Sales execution, pricing, retention, and positioning are almost always framed around AI. That leaves real white space for creators who teach those subjects directly, without making AI the centerpiece.
Another observation: genuinely personal stories are rare. Lara Acosta's post about anxiety, depression, and PTSD is one of the few examples-and even that ties back to a business lesson and reinforces her brand. Across the dataset, personal stories are used with a clear purpose, not just to share experience.

The repeatable playbook
If I were building a B2B SaaS content engine from this corpus, here's the playbook I'd follow. It's based on the patterns I consistently observed, not assumptions from the README. I wouldn't copy any single creator-I'd combine the patterns that showed up repeatedly into one consistent system.
1. Decide what you're optimizing for. Choose between insight-first (build authority and reach, keep promotion subtle) and offer-first (drive leads and conversions, lead content toward a CTA). The creators here are very consistent about which game they play, and that consistency is part of why their content works. Don't blend the two without a clear reason.
2. Use the reframe structure in every post. Name a common belief → challenge it → explain the real mechanic with a specific detail → end with a clear takeaway. This was the single most repeatable pattern behind high-performing posts.
3. Anchor every post in something specific. Include at least one concrete detail-a real number, a company, a name, or a specific example. Before publishing, ask: does this post contain at least one concrete detail that supports the argument? If not, strengthen it first.
4. Prioritize consistency over daily posting. The only frequency recommendation backed by data says posting more than 1–2 times a week can hurt average engagement. So I'd aim for two to four high-quality posts a week, making sure each adds value rather than posting just to keep a streak.
5. Match the CTA to the goal. If the goal is reach, end with an open question that invites discussion. If the goal is pipeline, use a direct CTA-comment or DM a keyword to get a resource. The strongest posts have one clear objective; they rarely try to start a conversation and drive a conversion at the same time.

A few notes on my approach
I read all 150 posts in full, not a sample. The percentages in the earlier patterns are my own estimates from reading the whole dataset, since the repository doesn't include metadata for things like post format.
Wherever the README and the dataset didn't align, I based my conclusions on the evidence in the posts. I couldn't verify the carousel statistic, the engagement KPIs, or the fixed 60/25/15 content mix from the corpus, so I chose not to treat those as confirmed findings.
The strongest evidence in the dataset comes from Mark Roberge's third post, which references Arjun Moorthy's study of 6,753 LinkedIn posts. If I were extending this analysis, that's the source I'd explore next, because it's the only large-scale, data-backed reference in the corpus.
